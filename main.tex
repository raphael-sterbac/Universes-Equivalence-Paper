\begin{abstract}
    In this paper, we present a modular and algorithmic proof for the equivalence of different
    presentations of type theory, notably relating Russell and Tarski presentations for a cumulative
    hierarchy of universes. More generally, we relate generalised algebraic and pure type system formulations, bridging
    the gap between the type theory used to prove metatheoretical results, and the one used by practicioners.
    Our work can also be seen as an extension of initiality results to more informal presentations of type theory.
\end{abstract}


\section{Introduction}

In type theory, universes allow to manipulate types as terms, and to avoid Girard's paradox, one often introduces a \textit{hierarchy} of universes:
\[
    \U_0, \U_1, \U_2, ...
\]

One can ask those universes to be \textit{cumulative}, asking for inclusions between them. Concretely, this means that if we have
a term $a : \U_n$, then we should be able to "lift" it to $\U_m$ for $m > n$. This can be done implicitly, such that $a: \U_m$ as well, 
or with an explicit coercion operation  $t_n^m(a) : \U_m$. 

There are two different ways to formulate the notion of universe in type theory. Martin-Löf’s
first versions \cite{MARTINLOF75, MARTINLOF82} were formulated à la Russell, where a universe is seen as a type of types. Later, an alternative version à la Tarski \cite{MARTINLOF84}, where a universe is seen as a type of codes along with a decoding function, was also introduced. Usually, Russell universes are regarded as an informal notation of the latter, as \cite{Luo} suggested.

When presenting Type Theory as a Generalised Algebraic Theory, Tarski universes are mandatory, and one can show that the system à la Tarski presents the initial model of such a theory.
This initial model has been constructed explicitly in \cite{bcde2026}, following the method presented in \cite{Streicher} and formalised in \cite{Brunerie}. 
However, there are as many initiality theorems as there are presentations of type theory, and if people often assume that they
extend easily to the more informal syntax used in practice, this comes from misconceptions as they depend on subtle details of the presentation.
To solve this issue, Voevodsky proposed the \textit{Initiality Conjecture project}, which aimed at developing rigorous metatheory for large class of theories.
A first step at solving this goal was achieved by Streicher \cite{Streicher}, proving initiality for a fully annotated Calculus of Constructions, and then
proving its equivalence with the more common, unannotated formulation, close to a Pure Type System. 

Generalised Algebraic Theories (GATs) provide a framework to describe type theories and their model in a uniform
way, and to prove initiality rather easily \cite{noteGAT, Brunerie}, 
thus solving the first part of the problem. However, GATs give rise to a fully explicit and annotated presentation of type theory, which is inconvenient to work in.
Therefore, it would be essential to extend Streicher's equivalence results to relate Generalised Algebraic and Pure Type System presentations, where the latter is presented
with Russell universes and unannotated syntax.  This would justify rigorously the common practice of using informal syntax and Russell universes, as proposed by \citet{Luo},
and would legitimate interpreting such syntax in usual models of type theory.

We shall here show that Streicher’s technique can be extended to show the equivalence of the Russell and Tarski formulations of a hierarchy of universes.\\

\textit{\textbf{Related work.}} In recent work, Assaf and Thiré \cite{assaf,thire} show the equivalence between Russell and Tarski presentation of universes for the Calculus
of Constructions with $\beta$ as an external reduction. Unfortunately, their arguments do not apply to
formulations of type theory with judgemental equality, and do not extend to $\eta$-equivalence. 

Therefore, their proofs and results do not apply directly to our setting, and cannot be adapted easily, legitimating
the study of a new proof technique. Still, the work of Assaf \cite{assaf}
provides an extensive study of the syntax, and lists the structural equations necessary to achieve the equivalence, by achieving what 
he calls \textit{full reflection}. 

Cumulativity with a structural sub-typing judgement $A \leq B$ \cite{assaf,thire,Luo}
can lead to discrepancy between Russell and Tarski universes in a more general setting, as pointed out by Luo \cite{Luo}.
In fact, when adding inductive types such as a the type of lists $\lst(.)$, the sub-typing relation propagates
along the new type constructor: \[A \leq B \implies \lst(A) \leq \lst(B)\]
Concretely, when $\leq$ is the cumulativity relation, this means that we get $\lst(\U_0) \leq \lst(\U_1)$. This already breaks
canonicity as we can coerce $\mathsf{nil}_{\U_0} : \lst(\U_1)$, but we do not have $\mathsf{nil}_{\U_0} = \mathsf{nil}_{\U_1}$,
unless we manually add the equation.
Moreover, sub-typing does not hold for $\Pi$-types in the Set model, or it is at least unclear
depending on the encoding of application in the ambient set theory. 
Therefore, we believe that such a sub-typing relation is not desirable for our considerations.

Yet, structural sub-typing would provide a more convenient manipulation of universes in a practical proof
assistant. Notably, it simplifies greatly the use of algebraic hierarchies \cite{subtyping}.
The recent work of \citet{AdapTT} presents a general framework which would provide automatically the necessary
equations to solve the issues mentioned above. In any case, we believe that our work is orthogonal to such considerations,
and that our proof should extend to any system that handles structural sub-typing in a correct way.\\

\textit{\textbf{Contributions.}} In this paper we show the equivalence of Russell and Tarski universes by a method due to \citet{Streicher},
for Martin Löf's Type Theory with dependent products, cumulative universes and judgmental $\beta\eta$ equality.
Our proof also adapts to a theory with universe polymorphism and internal universe levels \cite{polymorphism}.
More generally, we relate the Generalised Algebraic presentation of type theory to the implicit, and unannotated presentation
inherited from Pure Type Systems. In particular, our resulte judtifies the practice of working in implicit
syntax, while legitimating the interpretation of, unannotated, à la Russell, implicit systems in usual models
of type theory, such as in Categories with Families.

The main argument is as follows: there is a natural forgetful map from the Tarski to the
Russell presentation, and one can show that any Russell judgement can be lifted in a unique way
to a Tarski judgement. This work is done in section \ref{equivalence}.

We rely on basic (and known) properties like no confusion and injectivity of type constructors for the generalised algebraic theory. Those 
properties will propagate along our equivalence results, allowing us to prove them only once.
Notably, they are a direct consequence of normalisation, which can be proven easily for the GAT, by an adaptation of the gluing argument \cite{Coq19},
of which we detail the construction for universes in section \ref{normalisation}.

Those properties seem to be strongly related to conversion \cite{meven}. In fact, our equivalence result itself is to be
related to the elaboration in proof assistants, as we exhibit a translation from implicit user syntax to an explicit core syntax.

Our proof is elementary and provides an algorithm to translate from one presentation to the other. Notably,
we only rely on the basic induction principles for the syntax, and not on stronger induction principles given
by normalisation. In addition, we formalised and verified our equivalence result between Tarski and Russell universes in Rocq \cite{Formalisation}.
Throughout the paper, we provide links to the corresponding definitions and theorems
in the formalisation, indicated by the Rocq logo \includegraphics[height=1em]{icon-rocq-blue-eps-converted-to.pdf}.

\section{Syntax and theories}

\subsection{The generalised algebraic theory}\label{GAT}

In this section, we define the theory with Tarski-style universes as a generalized algebraic theory, which both
presents the syntax in an algebraic way, and provides a canonical notion of model in categories with families (CwF). Those
were originally described by \citet{Cartmell}, and extended with dependent products and universes by \citet{CWF-CCD}. The GAT
described here is really close to the one of \citet{bcde2026}.

Our Generalised Algebraic Theory of Categories with families has four sorts: contexts, substitutions, types, and elements: 
\begin{align*}
    &\vdash \ctx\\
    \Delta, \Gamma : \ctx &\vdash \Delta \rightarrow \Gamma\\
    \Gamma : \ctx &\vdash \Type(\Gamma)\\
    \Gamma : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma) &\vdash \Elem(\Gamma,A)\\
\end{align*}

It also has operations making $\ctx$ a category (of contexts) which acts on types and terms.

\begin{definition}[Substitutions]
    It has a composition and identity operation: 
    \begin{align*}
        \Gamma : \ctx &\vdash \Id_\Gamma : \Gamma \rightarrow \Gamma\\
        \Theta, \Delta, \Gamma : \ctx, \sigma : \Delta \rightarrow \Gamma, \delta : \Theta \rightarrow \Delta &\vdash \sigma\delta : \Theta \rightarrow \Gamma
    \end{align*}

    which satisfies the following equations (we omit the contexts for the sake of simplicity) :

    \vspace{1em}

    \begin{minipage}{0.40\linewidth}
        \centering
        $\sigma \; \Id_\Gamma = \Id_\Gamma \; \sigma = \sigma$
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.40\linewidth}
        \centering
        $(\theta \sigma)\delta = \theta (\sigma \delta)$
    \end{minipage}

    \vspace{1em}

    We also require a terminal object in this category, written $1$ and corresponding to the empty context:
    \begin{align*} 
        &\vdash 1 : \ctx\\
        \Gamma &\vdash ()_\Gamma : \Gamma \rightarrow 1
    \end{align*} 
\end{definition}

Subsitutions are acting on types and elements, in the sense of the following definition.

\begin{definition}[Action of substitutions on types and terms]
    Our GAT has the following operations:
    \begin{align*}
    \Gamma : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma), \sigma : \Delta \rightarrow \Gamma &\vdash A \sigma : \Type(\Gamma)\\
    \Gamma : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma), \sigma : \Delta \rightarrow \Gamma, a: \Elem(\Gamma,A) &\vdash a \sigma : \Elem(\Delta, A\sigma)
    \end{align*}

    Satisfying the following equations:

    \vspace{1em}

    \begin{minipage}{0.20\linewidth}
        $A \; \Id = A$
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.20\linewidth}
        $(A\sigma)\delta = A(\sigma \delta)$
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.20\linewidth}
        $a \; \Id = a$
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.20\linewidth}
        $(a\sigma)\delta = a (\sigma \delta)$
    \end{minipage}

\end{definition}

We also need context extension, in the sense of the following.

\begin{definition}[Projections]
    Our GAT has the following operations:
    \begin{align*}
        \Gamma : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma) &\vdash \Gamma.A : \ctx\\
        \Gamma, \Delta : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma), \sigma : \Delta \rightarrow \Gamma, a: \Elem(\Gamma,A) &\vdash (\sigma,a) : \Delta  \rightarrow \Gamma.A\\
        \Gamma : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma) &\vdash \p_{\Gamma,A} : \Gamma.A \rightarrow \Gamma \\
        \Gamma : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma) &\vdash \q_{\Gamma,A} : \Elem(\Gamma.A, A\p_{\Gamma,A})
    \end{align*}

    Satisfying the following equations:
    
    \vspace{1em}

    \begin{minipage}{0.20\linewidth}
        $\p(\sigma,a) = \sigma$
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.20\linewidth}
            $\q(\sigma,a) = a$
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.20\linewidth}
            $(\sigma,a)\delta = (\sigma \delta, a\delta)$
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.20\linewidth}
            $(\p,\q) = \Id$
    \end{minipage}

\end{definition}

All of this defines an \textit{explicit} substitution calculus, from which
we can recover the usual presentation of substition, as defined in the following.

\begin{definition}[Usual syntax for substitutions]
    If $a : \Elem(\Gamma,A)$, we write $[a] = (\Id,a) : \Gamma \rightarrow \Gamma.A$.
    We also define $\sigma^+ = (\sigma\p,\q) : \Delta.A\sigma \rightarrow \Gamma.A$ for a
    substitution $\sigma : \Delta \rightarrow \Gamma$ and a type $A : \Type(\Gamma)$. This
    extends our substitution $\sigma$ to the extended context $\Gamma.A$.
\end{definition}

Now, we can add logical structure by requiring type connectives in our theory, starting with dependent products.

\begin{definition}[Dependent product]
     We require the following operations, corresponding to the dependent product with lambda abstraction and application:
     \begin{align*}
         \Gamma : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma), B : \Type(\Gamma.A) &\vdash \Pi(A,B) : \Type(\Gamma)\\
         \Gamma : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma), B : \Type(\Gamma.A), b : \Elem(\Gamma.A,B) &\vdash \lambda(A,B,b) : \Elem(\Gamma,\Pi(A,B))\\
         \Gamma : \ctx, A : \Type(\Gamma), B : \Type(\Gamma.A), f : \Elem(\Gamma, \Pi(A,B)), a : \Elem(\Gamma,A) &\vdash \app(A,B,f,a) : \Elem(\Gamma,B[a])
     \end{align*}

    Those should be functorial and satisfy the $\beta$ and $\eta$ rules, given by the following equations.

    \noindent
    \begin{minipage}{0.40\linewidth}
        \begin{gather*}
            \Pi(A, B)\sigma = \Pi(A\sigma, B \sigma^+)\\
            \lambda(A,B,b)\sigma = \lambda(A\sigma,B\sigma^+,b\sigma^+)\\
            \app(A,B,(\lambda(A,B,b),a)) = b[a]
        \end{gather*}
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.40\linewidth}
        \begin{gather*}
            c = \lambda(A,B, \app(A\p,B\p^+,c\p,\q))\\
            \app(A,B,c,a)\sigma = \app(A\sigma,B\sigma^+,c\sigma,a\sigma)
        \end{gather*}
    \end{minipage}

\end{definition}

We now add cumulative universes to our theory, by requiring a countable hierarchy of universes $\U_0, \U_1,...$
along with explicit codes, lifts and decoding operations. This presentation of universes is referred to as \textit{à la Tarski}.
This explicit system is the standard way to present type theory with cumulative universes in a (generalised) algebraic way.

\begin{definition}[À la Tarski universes]
    In our Generalised Algebraic Theory, we add a countable list of universes $\U_n$ with a decoding operation $T_n(a)$
    which takes  a code (i.e. an element of a universe) $a :\Elem(\Gamma,\U_n)$ and produces a type.

    We also close each universe under all the type connectives. Namely, we require
    $\Pi^n \; A \; B$ for $n \geq 0$, which are codes for $\Pi$-types in $\U_n$, alongside with
    $\U^k_n$ which are codes for $\U_k$ in $\U_n$ (with $k < n$). 

    Cumulativity is encoded by a lifting operation $t_n^k$ that takes a code in $\U_k$, and produces a code in $\U_n$ for $k < n$. To summarize,
    we need all the following operations:

    \begin{align*}
            \Gamma : \ctx &\vdash (\U_l)_\Gamma : \Type(\Gamma)\\
            \Gamma : \ctx &\vdash (\U_l^m)_\Gamma : \Elem(\Gamma,(\U_m)_\Gamma) \\
            \Gamma : \ctx, a : \Elem(\Gamma,(\U_l)_\Gamma) &\vdash t_l^m(a) : \Elem(\Gamma,(\U_m)_\Gamma) \\
            \Gamma : \ctx, a : \Elem(\Gamma,(\U_l)_\Gamma) &\vdash T_l(a) : \Type(\Gamma)\\
            \Gamma : \ctx, a : \Elem(\Gamma,(\U_l)_\Gamma),  b : \Elem(\Gamma.T_l(a),(\U_l)_\Gamma) &\vdash \Pi^l(a,b) : \Elem(\Gamma,(\U_l)_\Gamma)
        \end{align*}
    The decoding and lifting maps must be strict and natural:

    \noindent
    \begin{minipage}{0.45\linewidth}
        \begin{gather*}
            T_l(t_n^l(X)) = T_n(X) \text{ for } l \leq n \\
            T_n(\Pi^n \; X \; Y) = \Pi(T_n(X), T_n(Y)) \text{ for } n \geq 0\\
            T_n(\U_m^n) = \U_m \text{ for } m < n 
        \end{gather*}
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.40\linewidth}
        \begin{gather*}
            t_n^p(t_m^n(X)) = t_m^p(X) \text{ for } m \leq n \leq p \\
            t_n^p(\Pi^n \; X \; Y) = \Pi^p \; t_n^p(X) \; t_n^p(Y) \text{ for } n \leq p \\
            t_n^p(\U_m^n) = \U_m^p \text{ for } m < n \leq p
        \end{gather*}
    \end{minipage}
\end{definition}

It is important to note that those equations have been carefully chosen so that this presentation is equivalent to the \textit{implicit} Russell universes.
In fact, those naturality and functoriality equations are already well-known and already appear in the work of \citet{assaf, thire},
where they are described as requiring "\textit{full reflection}".

All of this defines a Generalised Algebraic Theory of Categories with Families like with dependent products and a hierarchy of universes.
We can then construct the initial model of this theory \cite{bcde2026}, which correspond to the syntax of the corresponding type theory.
This initial system
will be called $\mathcal{T}_G$, and can be presented with more usual inference rules, while keeping substitution explicit.
We also consider the identical system $\mathcal{T}_T$, but with implicit substitutions.

\subsection{Other presentations}

Now, we introduce the other presentations of which we will prove $\mathcal{T}_G$ to be equivalent.
First, we detail the rules of the theory $\mathcal{T}_T$, which has implicit substitutions and exactly the same rules as the GAT 
of section $2$. 

The main goal of this paper is to relate $\mathcal{T}_T$ to the implicit formulation \textit{à la Russell} of cumulative universes,
which forms a theory that we denote with $\mathcal{T}_R$.
This presentation conflates types and terms, it has universes $\U_m : \U_n$ for $m<n$, and enables to deduce
$A:\U_m$ from $A: \U_n$ if $n<m$, with no codes or lifts involved. 

This system cannot be formulated as a GAT, as terms of a universe should be elements of the sort of types as well. This
could be done by adding a sort equation and a stratification of the sort of types $\Type_n$, but then cumulativity would
amount at asking inclusions between those sorts, which is not possible in a (generalised) algebraic setting.

Both $\mathcal{T}_T$ and $\mathcal{T}_R$ have dependent products, lambda abstraction and application, described in the same way, with the
following rules.

  \ebrule{

Now, for the theory $\mathcal{T}_R$ (Russell universes), types and terms are conflated, and thus
we should be able to deduce $\Gamma \vdash A \; \type$ from $\Gamma \vdash A : \U_n$. Conversely,
the intuition is that whenever $\Gamma \vdash A \; \type$ then we should have  : \[\exists n \in \bN, \ \Gamma \vdash A : \U_n\]

Instead of having codes for each of the type formers, we now close $\U_n$ under the type connectives. Note that, as we
consider \textit{cumulative} universes, we do not take the suprema of levels for the dependent product. Cumulativity itself
is expressed by stating the proper inclusion of universes : if $\Gamma \vdash A : \U_n$ then $\Gamma \vdash A : \U_p$ for $p > n$.

Finally, we also consider a system $\mathcal{T}_P$, which removes redundant type annotations in functional application
and lambda abstraction from $\mathcal{T}_R$. Thus, $\app(A,B,c,a)$ and $\lambda(A,B,b)$ becomes $c \; a$ and $\lambda_{x:A} b$.
This system is closer to the practical syntax of type theory, inherited from the lambda calculus,
and often referred to as the "pure type system" presentation (PTS).

In $\mathcal{T}_P$, we can also remove the judgement $\Gamma \vdash A\; \type$ to manipulate only universes,
in a completely Russellian style. In such a system, there is no top-level of types and a types is simply a term
of a certain universe.

For a full and extensive description of the systems, please refer to appendix \ref{Rules}.

\subsection{Forgetful maps}

Now, we relate the previously defined theories, by exhibiting forgetful translations from the more explicit presentations to the less explicit ones.

First, we define a morphism of theories between $\mathcal{T}_G$ and $\mathcal{T}_T$ which makes substitutions implicit.
To do this, we map substitutions in $\mathcal{T}_G$ to a list of "elementary"
substitutions in $\mathcal{T}_T$, as there is no composition in this system. Then, when translating the action of a substitution
on a term or a type, we can unfold that list to get a type formed by applying all those substitutions successively.

\begin{definition}
    We define $\fold(.)$ inductively by: 

    \noindent
    \begin{minipage}{0.40\linewidth}
    \begin{align*}
        \fold(\q) &= v_0\\
        \fold([a]) &= [\fold(a)]\\
        \fold(A \sigma) &= (...(\fold(A)\sigma_1)...)...\sigma_n\\
        \fold(a \sigma) &= (...(\fold(a)\sigma_1)...)...\sigma_n\\ 
    \end{align*}
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.40\linewidth}
    \begin{align*}
        \fold(\p) &= \p\\
        \fold(\Id) &= \\ 
        \fold(\sigma\delta) &= \fold(\sigma)::\fold(\delta)\\
        \fold(\sigma^+) &= (\sigma_1^+,..., \sigma_n^+)\\
    \end{align*}
    \end{minipage}
\end{definition}

Then, we define the following erasure operation, which erases the lift and decoding operation of 
Tarski-style universes, and sends codes to the corresponding type formers. Concretely, this operation
forgets about all the explicit coercions given by the presentation à la Tarski, and leaves everything implicit.

\begin{definition}[\formline{Erasure.v}{7}{Erasurefunction} Erasure]
    We define the erasure operation $|.|$ inductively by:

    \noindent
    \begin{minipage}{0.40\linewidth}
    \begin{align*}
        |\U_n| &= U_n\\
        |\U_m^n| &= \U_m\\
        |\app(A,B,c,a)| &= \app(|A|,|B|,|c|,|a|)\\
        |\lambda(A,B,b)| &= \lambda(|A|,|B|,|b|)\\
        |v_i| &= v_i\\
    \end{align*}
    \end{minipage}
    \hfill
    \begin{minipage}{0.40\linewidth}
    \begin{align*}
        |T_n(a)| &= |a|\\
        |t_m^n(a)| &= |a|\\
        |\Pi^n \; a \; b| &= \Pi(|a|, |b|)\\
        |\Pi(A, B)| &= \Pi(|A|, |B|)\\
    \end{align*}
    \end{minipage}
\end{definition}

Finally, we define the map $\strip$  which erases type annotations from a term of the system $\mathcal{T}_R$, and gives a term of $\mathcal{T}_P$.
This function is analogous to the one defined by \citet{Streicher}.

\begin{definition}
    We define $\strip$ defined inductively by :
    \begin{align*}
        \strip(\lambda (A,B,b)) &= \lambda(A, \strip(b))\\ 
        \strip(\app (A,B,c,a)) &= \strip(c) \; \strip(a)\\
        \strip(\Pi(A,B)) &= \Pi(\strip(A), \strip(B))\\
        \strip(\U_n) &= \U_n
    \end{align*}
\end{definition}

In section \ref{equivalence}, we will be proving lemmas  of the following form:\\

\textbf{Lemma.} If $\Gamma \vdash t,t' : A$ in $\mathcal{T}$ with $|t| = |t'|$ in $\mathcal{T}'$, then we have : $\Gamma \vdash t = t' : A$ in $\mathcal{T}$.\\

This is exactly the shape of the lemma proven by \citet{Streicher} in his book for the counterpart
of $\strip$ in his setting.  

As mentioned in the introduction, this relates to elaboration in proof assistants. 
Concretely, this lemma means that if two terms are syntactically the same in the implicit user syntax, then they should have the same
computational behaviour in the core system, and thus should be convertible.

In the next sections, we will prove such lemmas for our different theories. With those, we will be able
to construct uniquely a derivation in an explicit theory such that it erases to a given judgement in the implicit system.

\subsection{Normalisation for universes}

Let $M$ be an arbitrary model of the GAT described in \ref{GAT}. In the following, we write $\Type_M$ and $\Elem_M$ for
its types and elements. We introduce the sets $\Elem_N(X,A)$ of normal terms, $\Type_N(X)$ of normal types, and $\Neut(X,A)$ of neutral terms,
along with their interpretation $\langle.\rangle$. 

\begin{definition}[Neutral and Normal terms]
    We define $\Elem_N(X,A)$, $\Type_N(X)$ and $\Neut(X,A)$ for $X$ a telescope (syntactic context), and $A$ in $\Type_M(\langle X \rangle)$, simultaneously 
    by induction, along with their interpretation $\langle . \rangle$ :
    \begin{align*}
        \U_n &\in \Type_N(X) \text{ for } n \geq 0.\\
        \Pi \; K \; L &\in \Type_N(X) \text{ if } K \in \Type_N(X) \text{ and } L \in \Type_N(X.\langle K \rangle).\\
        T_n k &\in \Type_N(X) \text{ if } k \in \Neut(X,\U_n).\\
        v_0 &\in \Neut(X.A,A\p) \text{, and } v_{n+1} \in \Neut(X.A,B\p) \text{ if } v_n \in \Neut(X,B).\\
        \app \; K \; L \; k \; t &\in \Neut(X,\langle L \rangle[\langle t \rangle]) \text{ if } K \in
            \Type_N(X), \  L \in \Type_N(X.\langle K \rangle), \  k \in \Neut(X,\langle \Pi \; K \; L \rangle),\\
                                 &\text{ and } t \in \Elem_N(X,\langle K \rangle).\\
        \lambda \; K \; L \; t &\in \Elem_N(X,\langle \Pi \; K \; L \rangle[\langle t \rangle]) \text{ if } K
            \in \Type_N(X), L \in \Type_N(X.\langle K \rangle),
            \text{ and } t \in \Elem_N(X,\langle K \rangle).\\
        \Pi^n \; k \; l &\in \Elem_N(X,\U_n) \text{ if } k \in \Elem_N(X,\U_n) \text{ and } l \in \Elem_N(X.\langle T_n k \rangle, \U_n).\\
        \U_m^n &\in \Elem_N(X, \U_n).\\
        t_m^n k &\in \Elem_N(X, \U_n) \text{ if } k \in \Neut(X,\U_m).\\
        k &\in \Elem_N(X,\U_n) \text{ and } t \in \Elem_N(X,\langle T_n k\rangle), \text{ if } k \in \Neut(X,\U_n) \text{ and } t \in \Neut(X,\langle T_n k \rangle).\\
    \end{align*}
\end{definition}

With this, we add some notations.

\begin{definition}[Normal forms syntax]
    If $A$ is in $\Type_M(\langle X \rangle)$, we write $\Type_N(X)|A$ for the subset of normal types $K$ such
    that $\langle K \rangle = A$.
    Likewise, for a term $a$ in $\Elem_M(\langle X \rangle, A)$, we write $\Elem_N(X,A)|a$ for the subset of normal terms 
    such that $\langle k \rangle = u$.
\end{definition}

The interpretation $\langle . \rangle$ from $\Elem_N$ and $\Type_N$ to $\Elem_M$ and $\Type_M$ can be seen as quotient maps : from syntactical
normal terms and types, we associate the corresponding term or type, quotiented up to conversion.
Therefore, normalisation amounts to showing that those quotient maps are isomorphisms. To prove this, we
construct a normalisation model, which equips each type with a reducibility structure.

\begin{definition}[Reducibility candidates]\label{struct}
    For $T$ in $\Type_M$, we define $\Type'(T)$ the set of $4$-tuples  $(T',K,\q_T,\r_T)$ with :
    \begin{itemize}
        \item $T'$ in $\Elem_M(T) \rightarrow \text{Set}$
        \item $K$ is in $\Type_N | T$. 
        \item $\q_T$ is in $\Pi_{u:\Elem_M(T)} \; T'\; u \rightarrow \Elem_N(T)|u$. 
        \item $\r_T$ is in $\Pi_{k:\Neut(T)} \; T' \; \langle k \rangle$. 
    \end{itemize}
\end{definition}

Here, we only detail the constructions related to universes à la Tarski, which is the core of
our matter. In fact, we need to normalise lifts and decoding operations with the equations of \ref{GAT}.
Therefore, we need to embed the normal form of the decoding and lifting of a code inside the reducibility structure of
the universe. The remaining details of the proof are the same as in \citet{Coq19}.

\begin{definition}[Construction for universes]
    The following describes the operations related to universes in the normalisation model, including
    lifts, codes and decoding.
    
    The reducibility predicate of a universe is $\U_n'$ where $\U_n' a$ is the set of $5$-tuples $(a',a_0,A_N,a_l,\q_a,\r_a)$ such that :
                    \begin{align*}
                        a' &: \Elem_M(T_n(a)) \rightarrow \mathcal{U}_n\\
                        a_0 &: \Elem_N(\U_n)|a\\
                        A_N &: \Type_N|T_n(a)\\
                        a_l &: \Pi_{m \geq n} \Elem_N(\U_m)|t_n^m(a)\\
                        \q_a \; u \; u' &: \Elem_N(T_n(a)) | u \text{ for } u : \Elem_M(T_n(a)) \text{ and } u' : a' u\\
                        \r_a k &: a' \; k \text{ for } k: \Neut(T_n(a))
                    \end{align*}

    Now the normalisation structure of $\U_n$ is given by: 
        \begin{align*}
                \q_{\U_n} \; a \; (a',a_0,\q_a,\r_a) &= a_0\\
                \r_{\U_n} K &= (\Neut(K)|u,\ K, \ \lambda \; u \; u' \mapsto u', \ \lambda \; k \mapsto k)
        \end{align*}

     We can recover the normalisation structure of the decoding $T_n(a)$ for $a$ in $\Elem_M(X,\U_n)$, given the one for the code $a$ : $(a', a_0, A_N, a_l, q_a, r_a)$. 
     \[
         T_n'(a) = (a', A_N, \q_a, r_a)  
     \]
    
     Likewise, we recover the normalisation structure of the lifting $t_n^m(a)$ for $a$ in $\Elem_M(X,\U_n)$, given $ (a', a_0, A_N, a_l, q_a, r_a)$.
     \[ (t_n^m)'(a) = (a', \ a_l \; m, A_N, q_a, r_a)\]

\end{definition}

By instantiating this construction to the term model, which correspond to our initial theory $\mathcal{T}_G$, we can define a normalisation by evaluation function 
$\nf : \Elem_M(\Gamma,A) \rightarrow \Elem_N(\Gamma,A)$.

\begin{theorem}[Normalisation]
    For a context $\Gamma$, the quotient maps $k \mapsto \langle k \rangle$ and $K \mapsto \langle K \rangle$ have sections given by $\nf$. 
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Injectivity and No-Confusion}\label{properties}

In this section we show the injectivity of dependent product (in the term model), 
using a semantic argument in the normalisation model. First we need the following
essential lemma.

\begin{lemma}
    $\nf$ commutes with the dependent product operation.
\end{lemma}

From this, we deduce $\Pi$-injectivity :

\begin{theorem}[Injectivity of dependent product]\label{pi-inj}
    The dependent product is injective in $\mathcal{T}_G$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Let's assume that $\Pi \; A \; B = \Pi \; A' \; B'$ in $\mathcal{T}_G$. Then, we have 
    \[\nf (\Pi \; A \; B) = \nf (\Pi \; A' \; B')\] i.e 
    \[\Pi (\nf \; A) (\nf \; B) = \Pi (\nf \; A') (\nf \; B')\]

    Thus, as $\Pi$ is injective in $\Type_N$ (it is a syntactic operation),
    we get : $\nf(A) = \nf(A')$ and $\nf(B) = \nf(B')$, and so
    $A = [\nf(A)] = [\nf(A')] = A'$. Likewise, we also have  $B = B'$.
\end{proof}

To prove our equivalence results in the next section, we will also need the injectivity of universes, which
is stated by the following theorem.

\begin{theorem}[Injectivity of universes]\label{univ-inj}
    If we have $\U_{n_0} = \U_{n_1}$ in $\mathcal{T}_G$, then $n_0 = n_1$. 
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Let's assume that $\U_{n_0} = \U_{n_1}$. We can normalise this equation to $\nf(\U_{n_0}) = \nf(\U_{n_1})$,
    which corresponds to $\U_{n_0} = \U_{n_1}$ syntactically. Hence, we get directly: $n_0 = n_1$.
\end{proof}

We also get the "no-confusion" lemmas, which are a counterpart of injectivity of type formers.

\begin{theorem}[No-Confusion]\label{no-confusion}
    The following judgement is not derivable in $\mathcal{T}_G$: $\Gamma \vdash \Pi A B = \U_n$, for any types $A$ and $B$, and any level $n$. 
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    If such a judgement existed, we would have $\nf(\Pi(A,B)) = \nf(\U_n)$, which is impossible syntactically.
\end{proof}

With all of those result, we will be able to show that the equivalence of all our systems. 

\section{Relating our different presentations : equivalence results}\label{equivalence}

In this section, we show that all the systems $\mathcal{T}_G$, $\mathcal{T}_T$, $\mathcal{T}_R$ and $\mathcal{T}_P$ are equivalent. In particular,
we show the equivalence result between Tarski-style and Russell-style universes.

\subsection{Equivalence of $\mathcal{T}_G$ and $\mathcal{T}_T$ : explicit and implicit substitutions}

We show by an elementary proof that $\mathcal{T}_T$ and $\mathcal{T}_G$ are equivalent.
First, we would have to show that we can remove context annotations from the operations of 
$\mathcal{T}_G$ formally. This can be done with an analogous technique as for the equivalence of Russell and Tarski universes, and the main point is that the contexts and
types can be inferred from the derivation. We will not detail this proof here. 


\begin{lemma}[Well-formed definition of $\fold$]
    The forgetful map $\fold$ is well-defined.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    We check that $\fold$ produces types and term in $\mathcal{T}_T$. This is
    not completely clear, because of the cases $A\sigma$ and $a \sigma$.

    In fact, we check that $\fold(\sigma)$ indeed gives a list of well-formed elementary substitutions.
    Then, the successive application of those substitutions gives a syntactically valid type or term in $\mathcal{T}_T$.
\end{proof}

For the equivalence result, we need the usual substitution lemma in $\mathcal{T}_T$:

\begin{lemma}[Substitution lemma]\label{Substitution}
    If $\Gamma \vdash J$ in $\mathcal{T}_T$ and $\alpha : \Delta \rightarrow \Gamma$ is a substitution,
    then we have a derivation : $\Gamma \vdash J\alpha$.
\end{lemma}

With all of this, we show the following admissibility result :

\begin{lemma}
    The erasure of rules in $\mathcal{T}_G$ are admissible in $\mathcal{T}_T$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    The only two interesting rules are the one defining the action of substitution on types and terms.
    To prove admissibility, we iterate the substitution lemma when we unfold the list of "elementary" substitutions created by $\fold$:

\begin{mathpar}
  \ebrule{
    \hypo{\Gamma \vdash A \; \type}
    \hypo{ \vdash \sigma : \Delta \rightarrow \Gamma}
    \infer2{\Delta \vdash A \sigma \; \type}
  }
  \and
  \text{erases to:}
  \and
  \ebrule{
    \hypo{\Gamma \vdash \fold(A) \; \type}
    \infer1{\Delta \vdash \fold(A) \sigma_1 ... \sigma_n \; \type}
  }
\end{mathpar}

\vspace{0.5em}

Where $\fold(\sigma) = (\sigma_1, ..., \sigma_n)$, and this derivation is admissible with
$n$ applications of the substitution lemma. We do the same thing for the action of substitution on terms. 
\end{proof}

Consequently, we get the following correction result : 

\begin{theorem}[Correction of $\fold$]
    If $\Gamma \vdash J$ in $\mathcal{T}_G$ then $\fold(\Gamma) \vdash \fold(J)$ is derivable in $\mathcal{T}_T$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    By induction, we replace the rules of $\mathcal{T}_G$ by the admissible ones in $\mathcal{T}_T$ using the previous lemma. 
\end{proof}

Finally, we deduce that the two systems are equivalent :

\begin{theorem}
    The two systems $\mathcal{T}_G$ and $\mathcal{T}_T$ are equivalent.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    Each rule of $\mathcal{T}_T$ is trivially admissible of $\mathcal{T}_G$ because $\mathcal{T}_T$ is a subsystem
    of $\mathcal{T}_G$.

    Hence, we have the identity map from $\mathcal{T}_T$ and $\mathcal{T}_G$, and as $\fold$ acts identically
    on types and terms without substitution, we get our equivalence result.
\end{proof}

This proves the equivalence of $\mathcal{T}_T$ with the generalised algebraic presentation of type theory given by $\mathcal{T}_G$.
An alternative approach could use the initiality result of \cite{Brunerie} for contextual categories, which may be adapted to interpret $\mathcal{T}_T$
directly as a model of the GAT.

\subsection{Equivalence of $\mathcal{T}_T$ and $\mathcal{T}_R$ : Tarski and Russell universes}\label{sectionT1T2}

We now prove the main result of the paper : equivalence of Tarski-style and Russell-style universes i.e. of $\mathcal{T}_T$ and $\mathcal{T}_R$.
All the results of this section have been completely formalised in Rocq.
In the mechanized proof, we take the results of section \ref{properties} as Axioms, as the proof of 
normalisation is not the subject of this paper. 

First, we show that the erasure operation $|.|$ preserves derivations.

\begin{lemma}[\formline{Erasure.v}{64}{Erasurecorrection} Correction of erasure]
    If $\Gamma \vdash J$ in $\mathcal{T}_T$, then $|\Gamma| \vdash |J|$ in $\mathcal{T}_R$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
    By mutual induction, we prove the statement for our five different kinds of judgements : well-formation of contexts and types,
    typing, and conversion between types and terms. The proof is mostly direct, and amounts at showing that the
    erasure of each rule of $\mathcal{T}_T$ corresponds to an admissible rule of $\mathcal{T}_R$.
\end{proof}

Then, we want to show that this forgetful map has an inverse, hence proving the equivalence
of the two systems. For this, we will need some technical lemmas, which we all prove simultaneously by a mutual
induction on the derivation.

\begin{lemma}[\formline{Utils.v}{171}{Hypothesis} Hypothesis inversion]\label{hyp-inv}
    If we have a derivation $\Gamma, A \vdash J$, where $J$ is an arbitrary judgment,
    then we necessarily have a derivation $\Gamma \vdash A \; \type$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{lemma}[\formline{Utils.v}{168}{Hypothesis} Typing and conversion inversion]

    If we have a derivation $\Gamma \vdash a : A$ or $\Gamma \vdash a = b :A$ then we necessarily have a derivation $\Gamma \vdash A \; \type$.
\end{lemma}

Those two lemmas allow us to reason by inversion on given judgements, as Rocq's inversion
tactic would be stuck with rules such as :
\begin{mathpar}
  \ebrule{
    \hypo{\Gamma \vdash a : A}
    \hypo{\Gamma \vdash A = B}
    \infer2{\Gamma \vdash a : B}
  }
\end{mathpar}

We also need the following lemma, which will allow us to modify derivations given a conversion judgement.

\begin{lemma}[\formline{Utils.v}{182}{Hypothesis}]\label{Transform}
    If we have a derivation $\Gamma, A \vdash J$ and a convertibility derivation $\Gamma \vdash A = B$,
    then we can construct a derivation $\Gamma, B \vdash J$.
\end{lemma}

Now, we would want to state the main result, by adapting the arguments of \citet{Streicher}. One could try to prove the
following statement:\\

\textbf{Lemma.} If we have $\Gamma \vdash u_0 : A_0$ and $\Gamma \vdash u_1 : A_1$ such that $|u_0| = |u_1|$
in $\mathcal{T}_R$, then necessarily they are convertible i.e. $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = A_1$ and $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1 : A_0$\\

However, this fails when $u_0$ and $u_1$ are codes in different universes. For instance, we have $\Gamma \vdash \U_m^n : \U_n$ and $\Gamma \vdash \U_m^l$
which have the same erasure $|\U_m^n| = |\U_m^l| = \U_m$, but are not convertible (at least if $n \neq l$). Therefore,
we need to prove a more general statement that properly deals with the case when $A_0$ and $A_1$ are universes. In this case, we should

Even with this generalised lemma, we would get stuck in the proof for the case of the decoding. Indeed, if we have a code $a: \U_n$ type $A$ 
which erasure is $|A| = |a|$, then we cannot deduce much on $A$ itself as it could well be any type.
Nevertheless, we still know that $\Gamma \vdash A : \U_n$ holds in Russell-style because it is the same syntactic term as $a$. This should indicate that $A$ is a small type, i.e. a decoded type, and
in this setting we will prove that : \[\Gamma \vdash A = T_n(a)\]

Thus, we shall prove the following lemma, which is the most technical result of this paper. One of 
the reason for this, is that we want to keep the proof elementary, and only on basic syntactic arguments. We could also
have used stronger arguments such as normalisation, but this would make the proof less modular and algorithmic.
Still, we sketch such a proof at the end of the section.

\begin{lemma}[\formline{Equivalence.v}{809}{eraseinjty}] \label{mainuniverse}
    If we have two types $\Gamma \vdash A\; \type$ and $\Gamma \vdash B\; \type$ in $\mathcal{T}_T$ with the same erasure $|A| = |B|$, then they are convertible $\Gamma \vdash A = B$.\\
    If we have two terms $\Gamma \vdash u_0 : A_0$ and $\Gamma \vdash u_1 : A_1$ in $\mathcal{T}_T$ with the same erasure $|u_0| = |u_1|$, then we have two cases:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Either they are convertible $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = A_1$ and $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1 : A_0$.
        \item Or they are codes in universes, such that $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = \U_{n_0}$, $\Gamma \vdash A_1 = \U_{n_1}$, and 
            there exists two convertible terms $\Gamma \vdash v_0 = v_1 : \U_k$ in a lower universe, which
            lifts to $u_0$ and $u_1$ in the following way:
            \[\Gamma \vdash u_0 = t_k^{n_0}(v_0) : A_0\]
            \[\Gamma \vdash u_1 = t_k^{n_1}(v_1) : A_1\]
    \end{itemize}

    If we have $\Gamma \vdash t : \U_n$ and $\Gamma \vdash A \; \type$ such that $|A| = |t|$, then $\Gamma \vdash A = T_n(a)$. 
\end{lemma}


\begin{proof} (Of lemma \ref{mainuniverse})\\
    We prove the property by mutual induction on $A$ and $u_0$, proving the three statements at the same time. For each of the cases of
    the induction, we need a "catch-up" lemma which inverts the erasure hypothesis. Concretely the issue is that we cannot 
    deduce from $|u_1| = \Pi(A,B)$ that $u_1$ is a dependent product, as it could well be the decoding of a code $T_n(\Pi^n \; a \; b)$, or
    even a decoding of a lift $T_n(t_m^n(a))$. To solve this, we need to do another induction on $u_1$ for each case.

    We now present some of the key cases of the proof.\\

    \textit{Lambda abstraction.} If $u_0 = \lambda(C_0,B_0,b_0)$, then we necessarily have $A_0 = \Pi(C_0,B_0)$,
        and by inversion, the derivation $\Gamma \vdash u_0 : A_0$ looks like :

    \begin{mathpar}
      \ebrule{
        \hypo{\Gamma.C_0 \vdash b_0 : B_0}
        \infer1{\Gamma \vdash \lambda(C_0,B_0,b_0) : \Pi(C_0,B_0)}
      }
    \end{mathpar}

    Then $|u_0| = \lambda(|C_0|, |B_0|, |b_0|) = |u_1|$ and so $u_1$ is necessarily
    an abstraction or a lift. Now let's prove the property by induction on $u_1$.

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \underline{First case:} $u_1 = \lambda(C_1, B_1, b_1)$ with $|C_1| = |C_0|$, $|B_1| = |B_0|$, $|b_1| = |b_0|$.
    Also, by inversion $\Gamma \vdash u_1 : A_1$ is necessarily :

    \begin{mathpar}
      \ebrule{
        \hypo{\Gamma.C_1 \vdash b_1 : B_1}
        \infer1{\Gamma \vdash \lambda(C_1,B_1,b_1) : \Pi(C_1,B_1)}
      }
    \end{mathpar}

    Using the induction hypothesis on $C_0$ with $C_1$, we get $\Gamma \vdash C_0 = C_1$.
    Then, we can modify the derivation $\Pi_1$ using lemma \ref{Transform} to get a derivation
    $\Gamma,C_0 \vdash b_1 : B_1$. Now that the two terms live in the same context,
    we apply the induction hypothesis on $b_0$ with $b_1$, and get two cases. 
    
    In the first case, we get directly derivations $\Gamma \vdash b_1 = b_0 : B_0$ and $\Gamma \vdash B_0 = B_1$,
    and using congruence equations we obtain the desired $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1 : A_0$.
    
    In the second case, we get $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = \U_{n_0}$, from which we deduce $\Gamma \vdash \Pi(C_0,B_0) = \U_{n_0}$,
    which is a contradiction by lemma \ref{no-confusion}.

    \item \underline{Second case:} $u_1 = t_n^m(a)$, and we will show that this case is not possible.
        Indeed, we thus have $A_1 = \U_m$ and $|u_1| = |a| = |u_0|$ and we can apply the induction hypothesis
        on $a$, and in each case we obtain either $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = \U_m$ or $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = \U_{n_0}$, which
        are absurd because of lemma \ref{no-confusion}.
    \end{itemize}

    The case of lambda abstraction illustrated the main arguments used, and how no-confusion plays an 
    essential role to our arguments. However, in more complicated cases, we cannot always eliminate lifting
    cases.\\

    \textit{Code for a universe.} If $u_0$ is a code for a universe, then by inversion :
        
    \begin{mathpar}
      \ebrule{
        \hypo{}
        \infer1{\Gamma \vdash \U_m^{n_0} : \U_{n_0}}
      }
    \end{mathpar}

    Then $|u_0| = \U_m = |u_1|$ so we have two cases either $u_1 = \U_m^{n_1}$ or $u_1 = t_l^{n_1} (\U_m^l)$.

    \begin{itemize}
        \item \underline{First case :} We have $u_1 = \U_m^{n_1}$, and by inversion
    the following derivations : $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = \U_{n_0}$ and $\Gamma \vdash A_1 = \U_{n_1}$.
    If we define $k := \min{n_0,n_1}$ then we can construct derivations
    $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = t_k^{n_0} (\U_m^k)$ and $\Gamma \vdash u_1 = t_k^{n_1} (\U_m^k)$, with $\Gamma \vdash \U_m^k = \U_m^k : \U_k$.

    \item \underline{Second case :} We have $u_1 = t_l^{n_1} (\U_m^l)$, and by inversion the following
    derivations : $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = \U_{n_0}$ and $\Gamma \vdash A_1 = \U_{n_1}$. If we define $k := \min(l,n_0)$, we can construct derivations
    $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = t_k^{n_0} (\U_m^k)$ and $\Gamma \vdash u_1 = t_k^{n_1} (\U_m^k)$, with  $\Gamma \vdash \U_m^k = \U_m^k : \U_k$.
    \end{itemize}

    % TODO : other cases, decoding or code for Pi ?

\end{proof}



\begin{lemma}[\formline{Equivalence.v}{8}{simplifyinduction}]\label{Utile}
     Let's assume that : $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = \U_{n_0}$, $\Gamma \vdash A_1 = \U_{n_1}$ and 
     $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = t_k^{n_0}(v_0) : A_0$, $\Gamma \vdash u_1 = t_k^{n_1}(v_1) : A_1$ with
     $\Gamma \vdash v_0 = v_1 : \U_k$.

     Then if we additionally have  $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = A_1$, we get : $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1: A_0$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    By transitivity, we get $\Gamma \vdash \U_{n_0} = \U_{n_1}$ from which we can deduce $n_0 = n_1$ using
    injectivity of universes \ref{univ-inj}. Hence, $u_0$ and $u_1$ are codes living in the same universe.
    Using congruence of the lifting operation on the equation $\Gamma \vdash v_0 = v_1 : \U_k$, we get:
    \[\Gamma \vdash t_k^{n_0}(v_0) = t_k^{n_0}(v_1)\]
    from which we deduce $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1 : A_0$ by transitivity.
\end{proof}


Hence, we deduce the following corollary, which states that if we have two judgments
in $\mathcal{T}_T$ which are syntactically equal in $\mathcal{T}_R$ (when removing coercions, lifts...), then
they are provably convertible in $\mathcal{T}_T$. This already means that the Tarski system is not much stronger than
the Russell one, in the sense that if two term differ only by their explicit coercion, then they have the same
computational behaviour.

\begin{corollary}[\formline{Equivalence.v}{1121}{eraseinjtermplus} Judgemental injectivity of erasure]\label{uniqueness}
    If $\Gamma \vdash u_0 : A_0$ and $\Gamma \vdash u_1 : A_1$ in $\mathcal{T}_T$ and $|u_0| = |u_1|$,
    with the additional hypothesis $|A_0| = |A_1|$, then : $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = A_1$ and 
    $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1 : A_0$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
    We apply lemma \ref{mainuniverse} on the equality $|A_0| = |A_1|$ to get $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = A_1$, and on
    $|u_0| = |u_1|$ to get the two cases. In the first one, we directly have $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1 : A_0$,
    and in the second one, we use lemma \ref{Utile} to get the derivation.
\end{proof}

Now we prove the following theorem, stating the formal equivalence between the two systems:

\begin{theorem}[\formline{Equivalence.v}{1358}{sectionctx} Section of erasure]\label{equT1T2}
    If $\Gamma \vdash J$ in the system $\mathcal{T}_R$, then there exists a unique, up to conversion judgment
    $\Gamma_1 \vdash J_1$ in the system $\mathcal{T}_T$ such that $|\Gamma_1| = \Gamma$ and $|J_1| = J$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
    Uniqueness up to conversion follows directly from the previous lemma. We have to construct the derivation 
    by induction on the rules of $\mathcal{T}_R$, making an extensive use of the previous lemma for rules with two premises. Indeed,
    when using the induction hypothesis on two premises, we need to make sure that the same types have 
    the same translations. To illustrate this, we detail the case of the product.\\

    \textit{Dependent Product.} If the last rule applied in $\mathcal{T}_R$ is :
    \begin{mathpar}
      \ebrule{
        \hypo{\Pi_1}
        \infer1{\Gamma \vdash A \; \type}
        \hypo{\Pi_2}
        \infer1{\Gamma.A \vdash B \; \type}
        \infer2{\Gamma \vdash \Pi(A,B) \; \type}
      }
    \end{mathpar}

        Then, by the induction hypothesis we get derivations 
        $\Gamma' \vdash A' \; \type$ and $\Gamma'',A'' \vdash B' \; \type$ in $\mathcal{T}_T$ such that 
         $|\Gamma'| = |\Gamma''| = \Gamma$ and $|A''| = |A'| = A$. By lemma \ref{uniqueness}, we get $\Gamma \vdash A = A'$, and  the contexts
        $\Gamma'$ and $\Gamma''$ are convertible. We can thus transform the second derivation to get $\Gamma',A' \vdash B' \; \type$.
        And hence, we can construct the derivation in $T_1$ :

    \begin{mathpar}
      \ebrule{
        \hypo{\Pi'_1}
        \infer1{\Gamma \vdash A' \; \type}
        \hypo{\Pi'_2}
        \infer1{\Gamma.A' \vdash B' \; \type}
        \infer2{\Gamma \vdash \Pi(A',B') \; \type}
      }
    \end{mathpar}
\end{proof}

This finally proves that any judgement in Russell-Style can be lifted uniquely to a judgement in Tarski-Style, proving
the equivalence between $\mathcal{T}_T$ and $\mathcal{T}_R$ by exhibiting a section of the forgetful map.

To prove this equivalence result, we could have relied on normalisation to simplify the proofs, at
the cost of relying on less elementary properties of the theory. In fact, if we proved normalisation for
$\mathcal{T}_R$ as well, we could construct the derivation of \ref{equT1T2} by an induction on normal forms.
Then, by showing that the erasure map $|.|$ commutes with $\nf(.)$, we would obtain the equivalence result.

However, we chose to not rely on such results as they are stronger and not needed.
Notably, this makes our proof more modular, as it does not depend
on a normalisation by evaluation map, which would have to be constructed for each theory
to be considered.

\subsection{Equivalence of $\mathcal{T}_R$ and $\mathcal{T}_P$ : unannotated syntax}

Finally, we prove our last equivalence result between $\mathcal{T}_R$ and $\mathcal{T}_P$, relating
annotated and unannotated syntax. Concretely, the target theory $\mathcal{T}_P$ is the closest we can
get to a presentation as a Pure Type System of Martin Löf's type theory with a cumulative hierarchy of universes.
This section is a direct adaptation of the arguments of \citet{Streicher} to our setting. Notably, we need
to adapt the arguments because the theory $\mathcal{T}_R$ does not enjoy uniqueness of types, precisely because
of cumulativity.

Just as we did for the previous equivalence result, we prove that the forgetful map $\strip$ preserves
derivations.

\begin{theorem}
    If $\Gamma \vdash J$ in $\mathcal{T}_R$, then we have a derivation $\strip(\Gamma) \vdash \strip(J)$ in $\mathcal{T}_P$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    We just need to prove that the stripping of rules in $\mathcal{T}_R$ are admissible in $\mathcal{T}_P$, which
    is quite easy as each rule in $\mathcal{T}_R$ correspond directly to a rule of $\mathcal{T}_P$. Then, the theorem
    is proved by an induction on the derivation which replaces rules in $\mathcal{T}_R$ by the admissible ones in $\mathcal{T}_P$.
\end{proof}

Now, following the ideas of \citet{Streicher}, we prove the following lemma, stating that every term or type which
become syntactically equal after erasing the annotations with $\strip$, are convertible in $\mathcal{T}_R$. Here, 
types are not unique because of cumulativity, so this means that we could well have $\strip(u_0) = \strip(u_1)$ without
necessarily having $t$ and $u$ convertible. This is due to the annotations that are subject to cumulativity, like in
the following counter-example.

\begin{example}
    We have : \[\strip(\lambda(\U_n,\U_n,v_0)) = \lambda(\U_n,v_0) = \strip(\lambda(\U_n,\U_p,v_0)),\] but we do not have the conversion
    $\Gamma \vdash \lambda(\U_n,\U_n,v_0) = \lambda(\U_n,\U_p,v_0)$ if $n<p$.
\end{example}

Nevertheless, we would still get $u_0 \leq u_1$ where $\leq$ is the same relation as $\Gamma \vdash \cdot = \cdot$ but
only asks for a sub-typing relation on annotations for the congruence equations. % TODO: make this more precise
Therefore, we shall prove the following lemma.

\begin{lemma}\label{streichT2T2'}

    We have :
    \begin{itemize}
        \item If $\Gamma \vdash u_0 : A_0$ and $\Gamma \vdash u_1 : A_1$ in $\mathcal{T}_R$,
            with $\strip(u_0) = \strip(u_1)$ in $\mathcal{T}_P$, then $u_0 \leq u_1$ and $A_0 \leq A_1$.

        \item If $\Gamma \vdash A \; \type$ and $\Gamma \vdash B \; \type$ with $\strip(A) = \strip(B)$, then we have
            $\Gamma \vdash A = B$.
    \end{itemize}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}    
    Here, again we prove the two statements by mutual induction on $A$ and $u_0$, using
    the same proof technique as in lemma \ref{mainuniverse}. We detail the cases for 
    lambda abstraction and function application, which are the most interesting ones. \\

    \textit{Function application.} If $u_0$ is an application, then the last rule applied is : 

        \begin{mathpar}
          \ebrule{
            \hypo{\Gamma \vdash a_0 : C_0}
            \hypo{\Gamma \vdash f_0 : \Pi(C_0,B_0)}
            \infer2{\Gamma \vdash \app(C_0,B_0,f_0,a_0) : B_0[a_0]}
          }
        \end{mathpar}

        Then, $\strip(u_0) = \strip(f_0) \; \strip(a_0) = \strip(u_1)$, and
        thus necessarily $u_1$ is also an application $\app(C_1,B_1,f_1,a_1)$ with $\strip(f_0) = \strip(f_1)$, and $\strip(a_0) = \strip(a_1)$.
        By inversion, we also have the analogous derivation $\Gamma \vdash \app(C_1,B_1,f_1,a_1) : B_1[a_1]$.

        Then, using the induction hypothesis we get $a_0 \leq a_1$ and $f_0 \leq f_1$ with $\Pi(C_0,B_0) \leq \Pi(C_1,B_1)$, 
        from which we deduce $C_0 \leq C_1$ and $B_0 \leq B_1$. We conclude by using the (weak) congruence equation for $\leq$.\\

    \textit{Lambda Abstraction.} If $u_0$ is an abstraction, then the last rule applied is : 

        \begin{mathpar}
          \ebrule{
            \hypo{\Gamma.C_0 \vdash b_0 : B_0}
            \infer1{\Gamma \vdash \lambda(C_0,B_0,b_0) : \Pi(C_0,B_0)}
          }
        \end{mathpar}

        Then, $\strip(u_0) = \lambda (\strip(C_0)), \strip(b_0)) = \strip(u_1)$, and
        thus necessarily $u_1$ is a lambda abstraction $\lambda(C_1,B_1,b_1)$ with $\strip(C_0) = \strip(C_1)$ and $\strip(b_0) = \strip(b_1)$,
        along with the analogous derivation $\Gamma \vdash \lambda(C_1,B_1,b_1) : \Pi(C_1,B_1)$.

        We can prove lemma \ref{hyp-inv} for $\mathcal{T}_R$, and thus get 
        $\Gamma \vdash C_0 \; \type$ and $\Gamma \vdash C_1 \; \type$, on which we can apply 
        the induction hypothesis and get: $\Gamma \vdash C_0 = C_1$.
        Now, we transform $\Pi_1$ to a derivation $\Gamma,C_0 \vdash b_1 : B_1$, and apply the 
        induction hypothesis to obtain $b_0 \leq b_1$ and $B_0 \leq B_1$. We conclude using the congruence  $\leq$ to get $u_0 \leq u_1$ and $\Pi(C_0,B_0) \leq \Pi(C_1,B_1)$.\\
\end{proof}

Now we want to prove that if we ask
for the additional hypothesis $\strip(A_0) = \strip(A_1)$, then we obtain the desired $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1 : A_0$, which is what we wanted. In fact, this will allow us to prove
that $\strip$ admits a section, and that $\mathcal{T}_R$ and $\mathcal{T}_P$ are equivalent.

\begin{lemma}
         If $\Gamma \vdash u_0 : A_0$ and $\Gamma \vdash u_1 : A_1$ in $\mathcal{T}_R$,
            with $\strip(u_0) = \strip(u_1)$ and $\strip(A_0) = \strip(A_1)$ in $\mathcal{T}_P$, then $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1 : A_0$ and $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = A_1$.
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    We directly get $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = A_1$ by applying lemma \ref{streichT2T2'} on $\strip(A_0) = \strip(A_1)$.
    Likewise, we also get $u_0 \leq u_1$ by applying it on $\strip(u_0) = \strip(u_1)$. Now, we will show that we can reconstruct a derivation $\Gamma \vdash u_0 = u_1 : A_0$ by
    induction on the derivation of $u_0 \leq u_1$. The only interesting cases are the congruence cases, for which we have to show that we have a proper conversion for the annotations.\\

    \textit{Function application.} If $u_0 = \app(C_0,B_0,b_0)$ and $u_1 = \app(C_1,B_1,b_1)$ are lambda abstractions, then by inversion $A_0$ and $A_1$ are dependent products $\Pi(C_0,B_0)$ and $\Pi(C_1,B_1)$. Hence,  
    the equation $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = A_1$ is a conversion between the products, on which we can apply $\Pi$-injectivity (lemma \ref{pi-inj}) to get $\Gamma \vdash C_0 = C_1$ and
    $\Gamma \vdash B_0 = B_1$.
    By the induction hypothesis we also get $\Gamma \vdash b_0 = b_1 : C_0$ and we conclude using congruence equations.\\

    \textit{Lambda abstraction.} If $u_0 = \lambda(C_0,B_0,b_0)$ and $u_1 = \lambda(C_1,B_1,b_1)$ are lambda abstractions, then by inversion $A_0$ and $A_1$ are dependent products $\Pi(C_0,B_0)$ and $\Pi(C_1,B_1)$. Hence,  
    the equation $\Gamma \vdash A_0 = A_1$ is a conversion between the product, on which we can apply $\Pi$-injectivity (lemma \ref{pi-inj}) to get $\Gamma \vdash C_0 = C_1$ and
    $\Gamma \vdash B_0 = B_1$.
    By the induction hypothesis we also get $\Gamma \vdash b_0 = b_1 : C_0$ and we conclude using congruence equations.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{equT2T2'}
    If $\Gamma \vdash J$ in $\mathcal{T}_P$, then we have a unique up to conversion judgment 
    $\Gamma' \vdash J'$ in $\mathcal{T}_R$ such that $\strip(\Gamma') = \Gamma$ and $\strip(J') = J$.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
    By induction on the derivation $\Gamma \vdash J$, with the previous lemma, we construct a derivation in $\mathcal{T}_R$.
    The main point is that the typing annotations can be inferred from the derivation, and that the previous lemma gives
    us uniqueness for rules with two premises. 
\end{proof}

We can also relax the rules of our system $\mathcal{T}_P$ to remove "$A \; \type$" judgments, to obtain a
fully Rusellian presentation of type theory, with no top-level judgment of types. 
To do this, we observe that the following rules are redundant.

\begin{lemma}\label{ruleuniverse}
    The rules for $\Gamma \vdash \U_n \; \type$ and $\Gamma \vdash \Pi(A,B) \; \type$ are redundant
    because of the following rule :
    \begin{mathpar}
      \ebrule{
        \hypo{\Gamma \vdash A : \U_n}
        \infer1{\Gamma \vdash A \;\type}
      }
    \end{mathpar}
    % TODO : make this statement more precise, less handwavy i.e. proper translation
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
        For the rule :
        \begin{mathpar}
          \ebrule{
            \hypo{\Pi_1}
            \infer1{\Gamma \vdash C \; \type}
            \hypo{\Pi_2}
            \infer1{\Gamma,C \vdash D \; \type}
            \infer2{\Gamma \vdash \Pi(C,D) \;\type}
          }
        \end{mathpar}

        When we translate the premises, we get $\Gamma \vdash C : \U_{n_1}$ and $\Gamma,C \vdash D :\U_{n_2}$, with
        not necessarily $n_1 = n_2$. By taking $n := \max(n_1,n_2)$, and replacing the premises with
        the appropriate rules for cumulativity, we get  $\Gamma \vdash \Pi \;C \;D : \U_n$.
\end{proof}

Hence, we remove those rules in the system $\mathcal{T}_R$, and get rid of the judgement $\Gamma \vdash A \; \type$. 
We can also consider named variables instead of de Bruijn indices, to get a theory presented as a pure type
system, with the additional rule for cumulativity.

If we wanted to go even further, we could also remove the annotation of the variable from the lambda abstraction, again
following \citet{Streicher}. However to do so, we curcially need to rely on the full normalisation result,
as removing this annotation can lead to multiple non-convertible derivations for the same judgment, as shown by the following example.
    
\begin{example}
        We have two derivations : $t :\U_n \vdash \lambda \,x.x : \Pi(\U_n,\U_n)$ and $t :\U_n \vdash \lambda \,x.x : \Pi(\U_n,\U_p)$, which are not convertible when $n<p$.
        Then we can construct the same derivation from thowe two premises: \[t :\U_n \vdash (\lambda \,y.t) \; (\lambda \,x.x) : \U_n\]
\end{example}

\section{Extending the equivalence}

In this section, we illustrate the modularity of the proofs in section \ref{equivalence}. In fact,
we believe that the proof should extend to various flavours of theories, as long as they satisfy
the basic properties of the syntax mentionned in the previous sections. Here, 
we propose two extensions by adding data types to our theory and considering universe polymorphism.

\subsection{Adding Data Types}

We can extend the proof of normalisation to a theory with data types, from which we can still
deduce the  key properties of the syntax : injectivity and no confusion of type formers, just as in section \ref{properties}.

Concretely, let's assume that we have a datatype whose formation rule is the following. 
\begin{mathpar}
  \ebrule{
    \hypo{\Gamma \vdash \overline{c} \; \type}
    \infer1{\Gamma \vdash \textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}(\overline{c}) \; \type}
  }
\end{mathpar}

Then, it should also have a corresponding code in each universe, satisfying the corresponding equations
with respect to lifting and decoding.
\begin{mathpar}
  \ebrule{
    \hypo{\Gamma \vdash \overline{c} : \U_i}
    \infer1{\Gamma \vdash \textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c}) : \U_i}
  }
  \and 
  \ebrule{
    \hypo{\Gamma \vdash \overline{c} : \U_i}
    \infer1[($i\leq j$)]{\Gamma \vdash t_i^j(\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c})) =  \textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(t_i^j(\overline{c})) : \U_j}
  }
  \and 
  \ebrule{
    \hypo{\Gamma \vdash \overline{c} : \U_i}
    \infer1{\Gamma \vdash T_i(\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c})) =  \textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}(T_i(\overline{c}))}
  }
\end{mathpar}

This adds two cases to the proof of lemma \ref{mainuniverse}, one for the datatype itself, and one for its code. To deal with those them,
we need to prove the corresponding catch-up lemma for $\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(.)$, whose proof is always of the same shape.

\begin{lemma}
    If we have $\Gamma \vdash \textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c}) : \U_i$ and $\Gamma \vdash u : A$ in $\mathcal{T}_T$ with the same erasure $|\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c})| = |u| = \textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}(|\overline{c}|) $. Then
    we have $\Gamma \vdash A = \U_{j}$, and 
            there exists two convertible terms $\Gamma \vdash v_0 = v_1 : \U_k$ in a lower universe, which
            lifts to $\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c})$ and $u$ in the following way: 
            \[\Gamma \vdash \textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c}) = t_k^{i}(v_0) : \U_i\]
            \[\Gamma \vdash u = t_k^{j}(v_1) : A\]
\end{lemma}
\begin{proof}
    By induction on $u$, there are only two cases given the syntactic equality $|\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c})| = |u| = \textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}(\overline{c})$, and 
    $u$ is either a code $\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c'})$, or a lift $t_k^l(a)$.\\

    \textit{Code.} $u$ is a code $\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^j(\overline{c'})$, such that $\Gamma \vdash A_1 = \U_j$. We have $|\overline{c}| = |\overline{c'}|$, on
        which we can apply the induction hypothesis to get $\Gamma \vdash v_0 = v_1 : \U_k$ which lifts to $\overline{c}$ and $\overline{c'}$:
            \[\Gamma \vdash \overline{c} = t_k^{i}(v_0) : \U_i\]
            \[\Gamma \vdash \overline{c'} = t_k^{j}(v_1) : A\]

        By applying congruence equations of $\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^\bullet$, we get the desired result.\\

        \textit{Lift.} $u$ is a lift $t_k^l(a)$, such that the erasure equality gives $|a| = |\textcolor{Blue}{\textbf{D}}^i(\overline{c})|$ on which we
        can apply the induction hypothesis. We then conclude using the congruence of lifts.
\end{proof}

Using this lemma, the other results of section \ref{equivalence} hold and we can prove all the equivalence results between the corresponding theories.
Specialising this result to common inductive types, we notably get the equivalence result for Martin Löf's type theory with natural numbers, lists or identity types. 

\subsection{Universe level judgements and Polymorphism}

We want to extend the equivalence result to a theory where universes are indexed on internal levels, 
defined by a judgement $\Gamma \vdash i\; \mathsf{level}$ with a successor operation and a binary supremum operation, making
this new sort a sup-semilattice with an inflationary endomorphism \cite{polymorphism}.

Concretely, when we look closely at what was needed in section \ref{equivalence}, we observe that the only
thing that was necessary is to be able to take suprema and infima. In fact, the lower universe $\U_k$ of lemma \ref{mainuniverse}
is given by $\min(n_0,n_1)$ in the base cases. To reconstruct this operation, we observe that for any level $\Gamma \vdash n_0 \; \mathsf{level}$
and $\Gamma \vdash n_1 \; \mathsf{level}$, we either have $\Gamma \vdash n_0 < n_1$ or $\Gamma \vdash n_1 < n_0^+$. From this, $\min(n_0,n_1)$ can be obtained
from the internal universe levels by mapping it to $n_0$ or $n_1$ whether we are in the first or the second case.

Therefore, we can do \textit{exactly} the same proof as in section \ref{equivalence} by just
making the reasoning on universe levels internal, and we get the equivalence results.
In fact, we can even extend our proof to the full theory with universe polymorphism
described in \cite{polymorphism}, if we assume the basic properties of the syntax, as
normalisation for this system has not been proven yet.

In Agda, universes are indexed over a \textit{type} of universe levels such that universe polymorphism is
achieved using the ordinary functions of the type theory. The metatheory of such a system has been studied in the
recent work of \citet{norm-first-class}, giving a formalised proof of normalisation for this theory. Hence,
the basic properties of the syntax on which we rely hold for this system, and by doing 
the same discussion as before, we can prove the equivalence results for this theory, making the reasoning on
levels internal to the \textit{type} of universe levels.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}

To conclude, we proved that the GAT and PTS presentations of type theory are equivalent, and
that the latter is also initial as CWF models. Notably, Tarski and Russell 
presentations of cumulative universes are equivalent for a theory with $\beta\eta$
judgmental conversion and common inductive types, and this work extend with universe polymorphism.

Those equivalence result are fundamental to justify the common but informal practice of using implicit syntax
and Russell universes in type theory. Notably, this result allow to present 
Our proof relies on basic properties of the syntax of type theory, which are usually proven using normalisation,
but that we believe to be weaker. Indeed, our proof is completely elementary and modular. We believe
that it could be adapted to many extensions of the theory presented in this paper.
